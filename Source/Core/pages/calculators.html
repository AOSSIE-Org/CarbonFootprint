More Information

Carbon is cycled through ecosystems in several different forms. It has a tendency to combine with oxygen and form gaseous compounds, such as carbon dioxide (CO2) and carbon monoxide (CO), which in high concentrations can be considered air pollutants and play a role in climate change and global warming. CO2 can be removed from the atmosphere by trees through photosynthesis. In this process, plant cells convert the carbon from carbon dioxide to carbohydrates, such as glucose and starch, that can be stored in leaves, fruits, stems, trunks, branches and roots, and contribute to tree growth. Oxygen, upon which animals depend for survival, is released back into the atmosphere as a by-product of photosynthesis.

The chemical reaction for photosynthesis is:
6 CO2 + 12 H2O + photons ➔ C6H12O6 + 6 O2 + 6 H2O

The carbon cycle consists of the various phases through which carbon passes in living things, the soil, water and atmosphere. When the carbon cycle is in equilibrium, the rate at which carbon is removed from stores equals the amount being taken out of the atmosphere. The current concern is that the carbon cycle is considered to be out of balance due to human intervention.

The burning of fossil fuels high in carbon has disturbed the natural equilibrium and increased the rate at which carbon is returned to the gas phase. This increase in carbon in the atmosphere, particularly as carbon dioxide and methane, has been found to contribute to global warming through a ‘greenhouse effect’: gases trap infrared radiation in the atmosphere and cause the earth to warm.

Carbon constitutes approximately 50% of the dry mass of trees and when wood from these trees is used to produce wood products the carbon is stored for life in that product. It is only released back to the atmosphere when the wood product is burnt or decays. This carbon storage is estimated to last around 100 years in the wooden frames used in house construction, 30 years in furniture, 30 years in railroad ties and 6 years in pallets and paper.
